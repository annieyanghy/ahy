Problem

People are constantly distracted from important tasks or moments. Especially when working from home.

2020 is a difficult year, we work, study, and socialize from home. Thus, in my project, I worked on the issue of staying laser-focused for our tasks -- no matter it's work or relaxation. When the boundary of life is blurred, how to focus at the moment and create more quality time at home is a practice.


According to a survey based on over 2300 respondents in 2020 June, using social media (61%) and smartphones (53%) are ranked as two of the highest distractions for working-from-home professionals. Binge-watching is reported as the third, with 42% of respondents. A survey from Glassdoor also indicates similar concerns for distractions from TV.

Solution

An app that can nudge people to maintain the focus and start to focus, easily.

In this project, I will use the following psychological constructs and behavior economic theories to nudge users to perform the ideal behaviour.

Goal setting

frequently provide accessible feedback, break a difficult goal into smaller and more attainable subgoals

Priming

make the good habit a more accessible option for users to choose

Default bias

make the healthy option as a default so that it's more likely to be considered because it's an effortless option for users.

Framing

frame the good option as the more appealing one, so that users will prefer the framed, healthy (or good) option.

How It Works

1. Turn the calendar events into a focus timer

Behaviorial economics

  • Integrate existing goals and resources: Google Calendar or Microsoft Outlook.
  • Behaviorial economics: default bias and priming effect.
    •

    2. Break difficult tasks into smaller tasks

    Goal setting theory

  • Small wins and short-term goals are easier to focus on.
  • Subgoals feel more attainable than bigger goals.
    •

    3. Start a focus timer any time

    Behaviorial economics

  • Decrease the efforts needed to start a focusing space.
  • Making the better choice more cognitively available.
    •

    4. Collect feedback and progress from every focused minute

    Goal setting theory

  • Receiving accessible feedback frequently can increase motivation to for behavior changing.
  • Binding meaning to the goal.

    Design Process

    Research

    1. Literature review
    Key insights
    Distraction is driven internally and externally.

    Hypothesis formed: if I can minimize external distraction and strengthen the drive to stay focused, then it will be easier for people to stay on their tasks.


    2. User interview
    Key insights
    People do have the habit of scheduling, but they still get distracted due to stress, external distraction, or boredom.

    With the hypothesis formed, I reached out to 4 ideal participants for an explorative user interview. I prepared an interview protocol and listed out important research questions to ask.

    The goal of the interview is to understand people's usual focus habits, when or why they found they are most focused and unfocused.

    Interview findings
    100%

    of participants are prone to distraction when a task feels overwhelming.

    Feedback

    can motivate people to stay focused on a task.

    Attainable

    subgoals is crucial in nudging users to work on difficult tasks.

    Affinity map


    3. Intervention Causal diagram
    Intervention key components
    # 0

    Precondition

    Users are willing to integrate the app to their Google Calendar and remember to use the app when they need to focus.

    # 1

    Intervention

    Using a focus app to calculate their focused time, which can also connect to Google Calendar.

    # 2

    Behaviorial outcome

    User can focus on a task at hand without getting distracted.

    # 3

    Mechanism

    Visual indicator, feedback and goal setting can motivate users to stay focused.

    Complete version

    To plan the project to design an intervention to nudge users, I diagrammed different possible interventions to change users' behavior. This diagram is useful for me to think through the essential elements of adopting a new behavior.



    4. Competitor analysis
    Key insights
    Most of the apps offer timer, but none of them connects the digital Calendar, which we may have put our schedules on.

    Design

    1. Low-fidelity wireframe

    Based on the research finding, I created a very low-fidelity wireframe and turned it into a prototype.



    2. Lo-fi Testing and Card sorting
    Testing findings

    user wants some flexibility for a pause or a break.

    Calendar

    they love the feature of turning a calendar event into a focus timer.

    Visual

    visual indicator is important for counting down.

    75%

    of users think scrolling is more intuitive for time picker, instead of a slider.

    Goal

    (task breaker) is an ambiguous category in terms of information architecture.

    With the first version of wireframe, I conducted remote usability testing sessions via Zoom with 3 users plus 1 user for general user feedback.


    3. Medium-fidelity wireframe
    Key insights
    Users want more focus timer embedded in default settings.

    With the medium-fidelity mockup, I was able to ask for another round of feedback. To this point, the feedback I received is more specific for a component of a few features. For example, users want the focus timer being turned on when they initiate a calendar event from this app.

    Takeaway

    Nudging people into performing an ideal behavior is also a kind of user-centered design.

    My greatest takeaway from the project is the process of digging up psychological theories and behavior economics and intertwining them into the design. I feel a sense of achievement by knowing that this design is based on solid science and it will very possibly make a positive impact on users.
