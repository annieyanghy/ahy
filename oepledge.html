Problem

Low-income students often lack precious social capitals, which are valuable invisible assets to obtain a good job in society.

New York Times famous piece on the influence of racism on black boys. Original title: Extensive Data Shows Punishing Reach of Racism for Black Boys

Solution

The Opportunity Equity Pledge

With this Opportunity Equity Pledge, we wish to go back to the root cause of unequal opportunities -- why should we only value people with a particular type of education backgrounds while certain groups are deprived of access to these kinds of educational resources?

How It Works

1. Sign the pledge

to raise awareness

The Pledge is a starting point. We aim to provide the chance for people to take actions in the future starting from the Pledge.

2. Provide resources for future actions

to intervene in the behavior

We provide the website as a platform and offer a list of organizations for those who want to contribute more.

HMW

How might we inspire young, educated professionals (i.e. graduate students) to close the social capital gap?

Throughout our research, we found the methods to accumulate social capitals are not only about getting education or training on the low-income students’ side. Instead, it’s a collective effort on providing an equitable environment that the hiring side is also conscious about the inequality in social capitals and hence they are willing to make a change by giving different opportunities.

Target User

Diverse graduate students

We want to target graduate students due to their level of professionalism, life experiences, and level of reliability they can bring to students to foster building, briding, and bonding social capital.

  • As we have seen from prior research, people are more likely to succeed when they are connected with someone who looks like them and can empathize with their unique experiences.
  • Ideally, our target users would be graduate students who are close to their graduation and preparing to enter the job force, that way the pledge is fresh in their minds and a principle they can carry throughout their career.

    Research

    1. Legacy report

    We inherited a 20-page research paper from the team in the last semester, and we started to reframe the problem based on the paper. However, we found there are other discrepancies from what we believed is effective to help low-income students.

    First of all, this paper framed the problem around how we can help a low-income student get a student loan so that they can have better opportunities to get into a college.

    We argue that the problem shouldn't only be about "how to apply for student loan". Instead, we should step back and look at a broader narrative around low-income students.


    2. Literature review
    Intervention on the social class gap

    Then we came across this research paper of Professor Stephens, who conducted an intervention and has seen remarkable results years later. So, we decided to tackle the problem from this angle.


    3. Reframing the problem scope

    Throughout our first few meetings, we found nuances in the problem that we are trying to solve. Thus, we want to reframe the problem statement that's passed on from the legacy report.


    We want to see the journey of the problem we try to solve not only ends in college, but should prolong to find a job that can successfully help low-income students to move upward in the social ladder.


    4. Interviews
    Key insights
    Mentorship programs are a great idea to connect untraditional or low-income students with resources, but we need to think about we can influence them or motivate them further.

    Iterative Prototype

    Iteration 1 - A mentorship program

    We mocked up an online mentorship program and invited 4 users to attend.


    Iteration 2 - An Opportunity Equity Pledge

    After the feedback from the first prototyping session, we examined the form of our solution and thought about how can enhance the bonding for the participants.

    On the other hand, we realized that this multifaceted problem also needs extra work on the industry side that we need to remember to educate the society that we should give opportunities to everyone.

    Then, this form of mentorship has evolved from a program to a movement, that we want to make a pledge to our peers and people with privilege that we need to give chances to people without bias. This approach is influenced by the research of Professor Stephens.

    Feedback
    62.5% participants said they would sign the pledge.

    Takeaway

    If you are curious about details of my learnings, I wrote an article about for this class on my blog and on UX Collective. Overall, the highest takeaway is that design impact is not only about digital products. This semester, our team was working on a problem that combines education and poverty — the economic burden for the low-income students to receive higher education. Our goal was originally to use FinTech to solve the problem. For example, a solution to help disadvantaged students navigate through the process of a student loan application. For other teams, they were working on water quality and also FinTech as a solution to local fund. By setting these social issues as the problem, we had the chance to broaden our horizon about design thinking — it’s not only about wireframes, pixels or handoffs. It’s about how we approach a problem and how are open to the whole process of improving and getting closer to the core.
